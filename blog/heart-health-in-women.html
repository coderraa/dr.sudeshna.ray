Heart Health in Women: Common Symptoms Often Ignored

SR Dr. Sudeshna Ray · May 12, 2026 · 5 min read

Heart disease is one of the leading causes of death in women, yet it is still widely thought of as a "men's problem". That misunderstanding costs lives — because in women the warning signs are often quieter, and easy to dismiss as stress, acidity or simple tiredness.

As a women's health physician, I see how often cardiac symptoms are explained away — by patients and sometimes by clinicians. Women tend to develop heart disease a little later than men, frequently after menopause, when the protective effect of oestrogen falls. By then other risk factors such as high blood pressure, diabetes and high cholesterol may also have crept in. The result is a group of patients who are very much at risk, but who rarely picture themselves as "heart patients".

Why symptoms are missed in women

The classic image of a heart attack — sudden, crushing chest pain with a clenched fist over the chest — is drawn largely from how men present. Women more often experience symptoms that are vaguer and spread out over the body, which makes them easier to attribute to something else entirely.

Symptoms women should not ignore

  • Unusual breathlessness on mild exertion, or breathlessness that wakes you at night.
  • Pain or pressure that may sit in the chest but can also be felt in the jaw, neck, upper back, shoulder or arm.
  • Unexplained fatigue — a deep tiredness that is out of proportion to your activity, sometimes for days beforehand.
  • Nausea, indigestion or upper-abdominal discomfort that is repeatedly mistaken for a stomach problem.
  • Cold sweats, light-headedness or palpitations, particularly when they come together.
When to seek emergency care

If chest discomfort is severe, comes with breathlessness, sweating or fainting, or simply does not feel right — do not wait it out at home. Call for emergency help. With the heart, minutes matter.

Risk factors worth knowing

Some risks are shared with men — smoking, diabetes, high blood pressure, raised cholesterol, obesity and a sedentary lifestyle. But women also carry risks that are specific to their reproductive history. A history of pre-eclampsia or gestational diabetes in pregnancy, polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), and early menopause all raise long-term cardiovascular risk. These are exactly the kinds of details a gynaecologist is well placed to track over the years.

What you can do

The encouraging part is that a large share of heart disease is preventable. Knowing your numbers — blood pressure, blood sugar and cholesterol — is the foundation. Regular movement, a diet rich in vegetables, fruit, whole grains and good fats, stopping smoking, managing stress and protecting sleep all make a measurable difference. Where risk is higher, medication and closer monitoring can change the trajectory entirely.

    Key takeaways

  • ✓ Heart disease is a major cause of death in women, not just men.
  • ✓ Symptoms in women are often subtle — fatigue, breathlessness, jaw or back pain, nausea.
  • ✓ Pregnancy complications, PCOS and early menopause raise long-term risk.
  • ✓ Knowing your blood pressure, sugar and cholesterol is the first step to prevention.
